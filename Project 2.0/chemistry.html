Engineering Chemistry Syllabus

1. Introduction to Chemistry

1.1 Basic Concepts

Chemistry is the study of matter, its properties, and how it interacts with other matter. It provides a foundation for understanding the natural world and is essential in various engineering fields.

  • Atoms and Molecules: An atom is the smallest unit of an element, consisting of protons, neutrons, and electrons. Molecules are formed when two or more atoms bond together. The mole concept is a fundamental principle that relates the mass of a substance to the number of particles it contains.
  • Chemical Bonds: Chemical bonds are interactions that hold atoms together in a molecule.
    • Ionic Bonds: Formed when electrons are transferred from one atom to another, resulting in oppositely charged ions.
    • Covalent Bonds: Formed when two atoms share electrons.
    • Metallic Bonds: Characterized by a sea of shared electrons among a lattice of metal atoms.
  • Stoichiometry: The study of the quantitative relationships in chemical reactions. Key concepts include balancing chemical equations, understanding molar mass, and performing calculations involving moles, mass, and volume.

1.2 States of Matter

Matter exists in different states, primarily solid, liquid, and gas. The behavior of these states can be explained by kinetic molecular theory.

  • Gases: Gases have no fixed shape or volume. The Ideal Gas Law (PV=nRT) relates pressure (P), volume (V), and temperature (T) with the amount of substance (n).
  • Liquids: Liquids have a definite volume but take the shape of their container. Properties such as viscosity, surface tension, and vapor pressure are crucial in understanding liquid behavior.
  • Solids: Solids have a fixed shape and volume. They can be crystalline (ordered structure) or amorphous (disordered structure). The arrangement of particles in solids determines their properties.

2. Thermodynamics

2.1 Laws of Thermodynamics

Thermodynamics is the study of energy transfer and transformations. The laws of thermodynamics govern physical and chemical processes.

  • First Law: The law of conservation of energy states that energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed from one form to another. This law introduces the concept of internal energy (U), heat (q), and work (w) in systems.
  • Second Law: Entropy (S) is a measure of disorder or randomness. The second law states that the total entropy of an isolated system can never decrease over time, and processes occur in the direction of increasing entropy.
  • Thermodynamic Functions:
    • Enthalpy (H): A measure of total heat content in a system; it accounts for internal energy and the product of pressure and volume.
    • Gibbs Free Energy (G): Defined as G = H - TS (where T is temperature), it indicates the spontaneity of processes.

2.2 Applications of Thermodynamics

  • Phase Equilibria: Phase diagrams illustrate the states of matter at different temperatures and pressures. Understanding the triple point and critical point is essential in predicting phase changes.
  • Chemical Equilibria: The concept of dynamic equilibrium occurs when the rate of the forward reaction equals the rate of the reverse reaction. Le Chatelier's principle states that if a system at equilibrium is disturbed, it will adjust to counteract the disturbance.

3. Chemical Kinetics

3.1 Reaction Rates

Chemical kinetics is the study of the speed of chemical reactions and the factors affecting them.

  • Factors Affecting Reaction Rates: Several factors influence how fast a reaction occurs:
    • Concentration: Higher concentrations of reactants generally lead to increased reaction rates.
    • Temperature: Increasing temperature typically speeds up reactions by providing more energy to the particles.
    • Surface Area: Finely divided solids react faster than larger chunks due to increased surface area for collisions.
    • Catalysts: Substances that increase reaction rates without being consumed in the process.
  • Order of Reactions: The order of a reaction is determined by the exponent of the concentration of a reactant in the rate law.
    • Zero Order: Rate is independent of the concentration of reactants.
    • First Order: Rate is directly proportional to the concentration of one reactant.
    • Second Order: Rate is proportional to the square of the concentration of one reactant or the product of the concentrations of two reactants.
  • Rate Laws: The mathematical expression that relates the reaction rate to the concentration of reactants. The Arrhenius equation relates the rate constant to temperature and activation energy.

3.2 Mechanism of Reactions

  • Elementary Steps: Individual steps in a reaction mechanism. The sum of elementary steps gives the overall reaction.
  • Transition State Theory: Proposes that reactants form an activated complex (transition state) before forming products. The energy profile of a reaction shows activation energy (Ea) and the difference in energy between reactants and products.

4. Electrochemistry

4.1 Basics of Electrochemistry

Electrochemistry is the study of chemical processes that cause electrons to move, which is a key concept in energy conversion and storage.

  • Electrochemical Cells:
    • Galvanic Cells: Convert chemical energy into electrical energy through spontaneous redox reactions.
    • Electrolytic Cells: Use electrical energy to drive non-spontaneous reactions, such as electrolysis.
  • Nernst Equation: Relates the cell potential to the concentrations of the reactants and products, allowing the calculation of cell voltage under non-standard conditions. The equation is given by:

4.2 Applications

  • Batteries: Different types of batteries (e.g., lead-acid, lithium-ion) are analyzed for their construction, working principles, charge-discharge cycles, and applications in portable electronics and electric vehicles.
  • Corrosion: A process that leads to the deterioration of metals due to chemical reactions with the environment. Methods of prevention include coating, cathodic protection, and material selection.

5. Organic Chemistry

5.1 Hydrocarbons

Hydrocarbons are organic compounds consisting solely of carbon and hydrogen. They serve as the backbone for many organic reactions.

  • Alkanes: Saturated hydrocarbons with single bonds. Their general formula is CnH2n+2. Reactions include combustion and halogenation.
  • Alkenes: Unsaturated hydrocarbons containing one or more double bonds. Their general formula is CnH2n. Important reactions include electrophilic addition and polymerization.
  • Alkynes: Unsaturated hydrocarbons containing one or more triple bonds. Their general formula is CnH2n-2. Reactions involve similar addition reactions as alkenes.

5.2 Functional Groups

Functional groups are specific groups of atoms that impart characteristic properties to organic molecules. Understanding these groups is essential for predicting the behavior of organic compounds.

  • Alcohols: Compounds containing hydroxyl (-OH) groups. They can undergo oxidation to form aldehydes or ketones and dehydration to form alkenes.
  • Aldehydes: Organic compounds with a carbonyl group (C=O) at the end of the carbon chain. They participate in nucleophilic addition reactions.
  • Ketones: Similar to aldehydes but with the carbonyl group located within the carbon chain. They undergo similar reactions but do not oxidize to carboxylic acids.
  • Carboxylic Acids: Organic acids containing a carboxyl (-COOH) group. They can undergo reactions such as esterification and decarboxylation.

5.3 Aromatic Compounds

Aromatic compounds contain one or more benzene rings and exhibit unique stability due to resonance.

  • Structure and Stability: Benzene's resonance structures contribute to its stability. Huckel's rule states that a compound is aromatic if it is planar, cyclic, and has (4n + 2) Ï electrons.
  • Reactions of Aromatic Compounds: Aromatic compounds primarily undergo electrophilic substitution reactions, such as nitration, sulfonation, and halogenation, preserving their aromaticity.

6. Polymer Chemistry

6.1 Introduction to Polymers

Polymers are large molecules made up of repeating units (monomers) connected by covalent bonds. They have diverse properties and applications.

  • Types of Polymers:
    • Addition Polymers: Formed through the addition of monomers with unsaturated bonds. Examples include polyethylene and polystyrene.
    • Condensation Polymers: Formed by the elimination of small molecules (e.g., water) during polymerization. Examples include nylon and polyester.
  • Polymerization Processes: Mechanisms include:
    • Free-Radical Polymerization: Involves free radicals to initiate the reaction.
    • Ionic Polymerization: Involves ionic species to initiate polymerization.
    • Condensation Polymerization: Involves the formation of covalent bonds with the loss of small molecules.

6.2 Properties of Polymers

  • Mechanical Properties:
    • Tensile Strength: The maximum stress a polymer can withstand while being stretched.
    • Elasticity: The ability of a polymer to return to its original shape after deformation.
    • Plasticity: The ability to deform permanently under stress.
  • Thermal Properties:
    • Glass Transition Temperature (Tg): The temperature at which a polymer transitions from a hard, glassy state to a soft, rubbery state.
    • Melting Point: The temperature at which a crystalline polymer transitions to a liquid state.

7. Environmental Chemistry

7.1 Chemistry of Air Pollution

Air pollution results from harmful substances released into the atmosphere, impacting health and the environment.

  • Types of Pollutants:
    • Primary Pollutants: Emitted directly from sources (e.g., carbon monoxide from vehicles).
    • Secondary Pollutants: Formed by reactions in the atmosphere (e.g., ozone from reactions between sunlight and pollutants).
  • Sources and Effects: Natural sources (volcanoes, wildfires) and anthropogenic sources (industrial emissions, vehicle exhaust) contribute to air quality deterioration, affecting human health and climate.

7.2 Water Chemistry

Water quality is crucial for ecological balance and human health. The chemistry of water systems involves various parameters.

  • Water Quality Parameters:
    • pH: A measure of acidity or alkalinity; critical for aquatic life.
    • Hardness: Caused by the presence of calcium and magnesium ions, affecting water suitability for consumption and industrial use.
    • Turbidity: A measure of water clarity; high turbidity indicates contamination.
    • Dissolved Oxygen: Essential for aquatic organisms; low levels indicate pollution.
  • Water Treatment Processes: Methods include:
    • Filtration: Removes suspended solids.
    • Disinfection: Processes such as chlorination and UV treatment kill pathogens.
    • Purification: Reverse osmosis and desalination techniques provide clean drinking water.

7.3 Soil Chemistry

Soil chemistry deals with the chemical composition and properties of soil, crucial for agriculture and environmental sustainability.

  • Soil Composition and Properties: Soils contain minerals, organic matter, water, and air. The balance affects fertility and plant growth.
  • Soil Contamination: Heavy metals, pesticides, and other pollutants can degrade soil quality. Remediation techniques include:
    • Phytoremediation: Using plants to absorb contaminants.
    • Bioremediation: Using microorganisms to degrade pollutants.
